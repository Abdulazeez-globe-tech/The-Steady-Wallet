You've heard both names. The snowball says pay off your smallest balance first. The avalanche says attack the highest interest rate. The internet argues about this like it's a personality test, but the real answer is simpler than the debate suggests.

Here's the breakdown with actual numbers, plus my honest take on which one works for most people.

## The debt snowball: smallest balance first

List your debts from smallest balance to largest. Pay minimums on everything except the smallest one. Throw every spare dollar at that smallest debt until it's gone. Then roll that payment into the next one.

**Why it works psychologically:** You get a win fast. Paying off a $340 medical bill in six weeks feels like proof that the system is working. That feeling matters more than most spreadsheets will admit.

**The math example:** Say you owe $800 on a store card (22% APR), $2,400 on a personal loan (9% APR), and $5,100 on a credit card (19% APR). The snowball hits the $800 first, then the $2,400, then the $5,100. You'll pay slightly more in total interest than the avalanche. But you'll knock out two debts fast, and that momentum is real.

## The debt avalanche: highest interest rate first

Same setup. But instead of targeting the smallest balance, you target the debt with the highest interest rate. Minimums on everything else.

**Why it saves more money:** Pure math. Attacking the highest rate first means less interest accrues overall. On the same three debts, you'd hit the $800 store card first (22%), then the $5,100 credit card (19%), then the $2,400 personal loan (9%).

**The catch:** If your highest-rate debt is also your biggest balance, you might be grinding at it for a year before you close a single account. That's a long time to stay motivated without a win.

## My honest take

For most people, the snowball works better. Not because the math is superior. It isn't. But because the person who quits in month four saves nothing, and the snowball is harder to quit.

If your highest-rate debt also happens to be your smallest balance, the whole debate is irrelevant. You're doing both at once. Start there and stop overthinking it.

The only scenario where I'd push someone toward the avalanche: if you have a large, high-interest credit card balance (say $8,000 at 24%) sitting next to several small, low-rate debts. The interest on that card is costing you real money every month you delay. In that case, the math gap is too big to ignore.

## The part that matters more than either method

Pick one and start. The difference between snowball and avalanche over a typical $10,000 debt load is maybe $200 to $400 in total interest. The difference between starting and not starting is the entire $10,000.

Find extra money to throw at debt. The $47 in subscriptions you forgot about, the $90 you can shave from groceries. Where it comes from matters more than which debt it hits first.

## This week

Write down every debt you owe: name, balance, interest rate, minimum payment. Sort it by balance (snowball) or rate (avalanche). Pick one sort order and start sending extra money to whatever's at the top of the list. The rest sorts itself out from there.
