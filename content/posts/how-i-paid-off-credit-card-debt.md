I'm going to tell you the truth about paying off debt: it's boring. There's no single hack that makes $9,300 vanish. There's a plan, a spreadsheet, and a lot of months where you do the same thing over and over until the number is zero.

Here's exactly what those 19 months looked like.

## Where the debt came from

Three credit cards. The oldest balance was from a car repair I put on plastic because I had no emergency fund. The second was a slow accumulation of "I'll pay it off next month" purchases that I never paid off next month. The third was a medical bill I couldn't cover out of pocket.

None of it was reckless. All of it added up. That's how debt usually works. Not one big mistake, just a hundred small ones without a system to catch them.

## The math I started with

Total debt: $9,300 across three cards. Average APR: about 21%. Minimum payments: $215 a month combined. At minimum payments only, payoff would have taken over seven years and cost me roughly $5,400 in interest alone. Seeing that number was the push I needed.

## Step one: find the extra money

I went through two months of bank statements and found $290 a month I could redirect. Not painlessly, but honestly.

The grocery ceiling dropped from $360 to $260. That's $100 right there, mostly from meal planning and switching store brands on about a dozen staples. I canceled three subscriptions I'd forgotten about, totaling $42 a month. I set a hard $120 cap on eating out, down from about $200. And I sold a few things I wasn't using for about $350, which became a one-time extra payment.

## Step two: pick a payoff order

I went with the snowball. My smallest balance was $840. At $290 extra per month plus the minimum, I knocked it out in under three months. That freed up another $35 a month in minimum payments, which rolled into the next card. Momentum compounded from there.

## Step three: automate everything

The $290 extra payment went out the day after payday, automatically. Waiting for the end of the month never works. The money finds somewhere else to go. Automating it meant I never had to decide to pay the debt. The decision was made once, in January, and it kept making itself every two weeks.

## What I didn't do

I didn't take on a second job. I didn't stop spending money on fun completely. I kept a $60 a month "life happens" category so I didn't feel deprived every single week for 19 straight months. Deprivation budgets collapse. I've tried them.

I also didn't do a balance transfer. At the time, my credit score wasn't strong enough for a good offer. If yours is, a 0% transfer card can save hundreds in interest. But the card isn't the strategy. The extra payments are the strategy.

## Month 19

The last payment was $412.60. I remember the number because I'd been watching it shrink for three months. Clicking "pay" felt anticlimactic and enormous at the same time.

What changed after that: $505 a month that used to go to minimums and extra payments suddenly had no job. Some of it went to an emergency fund so I'd never need the credit cards again. Some of it went to savings goals I'd been ignoring. And yes, some of it went to a really nice dinner.

## If you're starting today

Write down every card: balance, rate, minimum. Pick snowball or avalanche. Find the extra money by scanning two months of statements. Set up automatic payments on payday. Then do the same thing next month, and the month after that.

The 19 months will pass either way. They can pass with the debt shrinking or with the debt sitting still. That's the only real choice.
