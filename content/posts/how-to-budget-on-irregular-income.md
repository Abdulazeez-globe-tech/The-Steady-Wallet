If you freelance, work shifts, or run a side business, most budgeting advice quietly assumes you don't exist. It starts with "take your monthly income" and you're already stuck. Some months you make $3,800. Some months it's $2,100. Which number are you supposed to plan around?

Here's the version that works when your paycheck won't sit still. I used it for two years of freelance income, and it's the only reason I stopped panicking every slow month.

## Budget from your lowest month, not your average

This is the part most people get wrong. They average their last six months of income and build a budget on that number. Then a slow month hits and the whole plan collapses.

Instead, pull up your last six months of income and find the lowest one. That's your budget number. If your worst month was $2,100, you build a life that runs on $2,100.

Everything above that is a bonus with a job to do. Not fun money. A job.

## Give every extra dollar an assignment

Say your baseline budget is $2,100 and a good month brings in $3,400. That extra $1,300 gets assigned before it can wander off. My order looked like this:

1. **Top up the buffer.** One month of baseline expenses sitting in checking. This is the single biggest stress-killer for irregular income, and it comes first.
2. **Fill the taxes bucket.** Self-employed? Move 25 to 30 percent of the extra into a separate account. April will find you either way.
3. **Feed the emergency fund** until it holds three months of your baseline.
4. **Then the fun stuff.** Debt payoff, savings goals, the boots you've been circling for a month.

The order matters. A buffer before boots, always.

## Pay yourself a salary

Once your buffer holds a full month of expenses, something lovely happens. You can pay yourself the same amount on the same day every month, like a boss who never misses payroll.

Client money lands in one account. On the 1st, you transfer your set "salary" to checking. Slow month, big month, doesn't matter. Your bills see the same paycheck either way.

It took me about four months to build up to this. Those four months were tight. The calm on the other side was worth every one of them.

## What to do in a genuinely bad month

Sometimes the low month is lower than your lowest. It happens, and it doesn't mean the system failed.

Drop to your bare-bones list: housing, utilities, food, transport, minimum debt payments. Pause everything else without guilt, including savings. The buffer exists exactly for this. Spending it during a rough patch isn't failure. It's the buffer doing its one job.

Then, when income recovers, refill the buffer before anything else. Not the vacation fund. The buffer.

## Start this week, not someday

Pull your last six months of income tonight. Find the lowest number. Write down what a month costs at that level, honestly, including the streaming subscriptions you forgot about.

That one number turns irregular income from a monthly guessing game into a plan. And a plan you can trust is the whole point of budgeting in the first place.
